OUTRAGE STRIKES RICE AS BACKPAGE MAKES “NEWS”

The Jan. 10 rendition of the Backpage stirred up controversy and ignited fierce debate around campus. Many heads turned as opinions proliferated; were the authors intentionally causing division or making a failed attempt at humor? The issue at hand, of course, was one of the most inflammatory ever covered: whether the Backpage had publicly discriminated against the largest demographic of the Rice student population by “jokingly” saying that Bakerites had access to the best servery on campus.

“I’m simply stunned that the Backpage would use its platform to privilege one individual servery,” one Roger E., who asked to remain otherwise anonymous, said.

“This publication clearly has an unrefined palette,” he said, adding that he would be better off eating “cold noodle soup” than understanding the “subtle contours of a cream-based black-pepper hollandaise.”

Chef Kyle Hardwick, executive chef at Seibel Servery, said he had similar concerns regarding the content of the Backpage.

“It’s appalling to me that the Backpage would publish such objectionable material in the current culinary climate,” Hardwick said. “It is evident to me that the editors only believe in two things: the First Amendment and Baker College Kitchen’s right to refuse service to non-Bakerites before 12 p.m.”

As the weekend turned, wild fervor on the Internet fanned the fire. Thresher Editor-in-Chiefs Drew Keller and Juan Saldaña described the online attacks as ranging from thought-provoking to hateful and unacceptable.

“[The comments were] a mixture of people saying that the ‘ad’ was promoting servery stratification and contrary to the vision and beliefs of James A. Baker, and then also just a bunch of anti-Baker slurs,” Keller and Saldaña said.

Rice’s Housing and Dining Office released a statement on Twitter condemning the “ad.”

“We are disappointed in the printing of an ‘ad’ that reduced other, equally-good serveries in an effort to put forward its own culinary agenda,” they said.

According to the author of the satirical “ad,” the intent of the Backpage was not to bait students into servery squabbling. “Look, I like food from all colleges,” the author said. “Baker food? Sure. North servery? Why not. They all contribute to a diverse culinary community, and that’s why I chose to go to Rice.”
